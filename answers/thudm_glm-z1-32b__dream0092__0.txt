## 5.2 Factoring x²+bx+c

We begin by noting that factoring a quadratic in the form x² + bx + c is the reverse of multiplying two binomials using the FOIL method. For example, consider the factored form (x + m)(x + n). Expanding this, we get:

(x + m)(x + n) = x² + (m + n)x + mn

This means that to factor x² + bx + c, we need to find integers m and n such that:

• m + n = b  
• m * n = c

This article presents a systematic and structured approach to factor such trinomials, starting with identifying coefficients and proceeding through various scenarios. We will explore different methods including the "ac" method, graphing, and completing the square, but our primary focus will be on the box method, which provides a visual and systematic approach.

### Box Method

Let's consider the quadratic 2x² + 7x + 6. To factor this using the box method:

1. Draw a 2x2 grid:
       [ ][ ]
       [ ][ ]

2. Place the first term (2x²) in the top left box and the constant term (6) in the bottom right box:
       [2x²][ ]
       [  ][6]

3. Find two numbers that multiply to 2x² * 6 = 12x² and add to 7x. These numbers are 3x and 4x:
       [2x²][3x]
       [4x ][6]

4. Rewrite the quadratic as 2x² + 3x + 4x + 6:
       2x² + 3x + 4x + 6

5. Factor by grouping:
       (2x² + 3x) + (4x + 6)
       = x(2x + 3) + 2(2x + 3)
       = (2x + 3)(x + 2)

This method works because the product of the diagonals is equal (2x² * 6 = 12x² and 3x * 4x = 12x²), ensuring the middle term is correct.

Another example: Factor 6x² + 25x + 14:

1. Draw a 2x2 grid and place 6x² and 14:
       [6x²][ ]
       [  ][14]

2. Find two numbers that multiply to 84 (6x² * 14 = 84x²) and add to 25x. These are 21x and 4x:
       [6x²][21x]
       [4x ][14]

3. Rewrite and factor:
       6x² + 21x + 4x + 14
       = 3x(2x + 7) + 2(2x + 7)
       = (3x + 2)(2x + 7)

This method is systematic and ensures students can factor even challenging quadratics accurately.

### Special Cases

**Perfect Square Trinomials:**

A perfect square trinomial takes the form x² + 2ax + a² = (x + a)². For example:

x² + 6x + 9 = (x + 3)²

To recognize these:

- The first and last terms are perfect squares.  
- The middle term is twice the product of the square roots of the first and last terms.

**Difference of Squares:**

Although not directly related to x² + bx + c but important in factoring, the difference of squares formula states:

a² - b² = (a + b)(a - b)

This can sometimes appear in more complex factoring problems.

**Dataset: Coffee intake**

Suppose we want to determine if the mean number of cups of coffee consumed per day by American adults is different from 2. A random sample of 40 American adults was asked how many cups of coffee they drink each day. The sample mean was found to be 2.1 cups with a standard deviation of 0.8 cups.

**Step 1: State the hypotheses.**

- Null hypothesis (\(H_0\)): \(\mu = 2\)
- Alternative hypothesis (\(H_1\)): \(\mu \neq 2\) (two-tailed test)

**Step 2: Determine the significance level.**

Let's choose \(\alpha = 0.05\).

**Step 3: Identify the test statistic.**

Since the sample size is 40 (which is greater than 30), we can use the Central Limit Theorem and conduct a z-test, though a t-test is also appropriate. However, with \(n = 40\), the difference between z and t is negligible. For simplicity, we'll use a t-test.

**Step 4: Calculate the test statistic.**

The formula for the t-test statistic is:

\[
t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{s/\sqrt{n}}
\]

Plugging in the values:

\[
t = \frac{2.1 - 2}{0.8/\sqrt{40}} = \frac{0.1}{0.8/6.3246} = \frac{0.1}{0.1265} \approx 0.79
\]

**Step 5: Determine the critical value or p-value.**

For a two-tailed test with \(\alpha = 0.05\) and degrees of freedom (\(df\)) = \(n - 1 = 39\), the critical t-value is approximately \(\pm 2.023\) (using a t-table or calculator).

Alternatively, calculate the p-value for \(t = 0.79\). Using a t-distribution table or calculator, the p-value is the probability that \(|t| > 0.79\). For \(df = 39\), the p-value is approximately 0.434.

**Step 6: Make a decision.**

Compare the test statistic to the critical value:

- \(|t| = 0.79 < 2.023\), so we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Alternatively, compare the p-value to \(\alpha\):

- \(0.434 > 0.05\), so we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

**Conclusion:**

There is not enough evidence at the 0.05 significance level to conclude that the mean number of cups of coffee consumed per day by American adults is different from 2.

#### **2. Significance Level**
